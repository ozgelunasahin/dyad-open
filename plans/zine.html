(03) — Chapter 01
What we have lost, and what social technology has cost us.
The promise and the betrayal

Platforms were built on a promise: connection, community, information. The promise was real. The betrayal came in the incentive structure that powered it.

Attention as infrastructure

The attention economy did not sell us worse products. It sold a version of reality optimised for engagement. Outrage travels faster than nuance. The infrastructure shaped the culture.

The loneliness paradox

We are the most connected generation in history. We are also among the loneliest. These two facts are not a contradiction. They are the same fact.

Lost.

(04) — The Cost
"We designed for growth. We got scale. We lost depth."

What we cannot see because we have never had it

The hardest thing to mourn is what was never built. We know what social media took from us — context, nuance, the slow accumulation of real trust. We have less language for what we never got to build instead.

What would public digital infrastructure look like if it were designed for depth rather than scale? For relationship rather than reach? For community rather than audience?

The thing about depth

A face-to-face conversation takes about an hour. You cannot optimise it. You cannot scroll past the uncomfortable parts. The format is the constraint — and the constraint is the point.

Depth is not a niche preference. It is what humans are designed for. We just stopped building for it.

(05) — Chapter 02
The landscape we inherit, and why building from within it matters.

Berlin as a site

Berlin is not neutral. It is a city built and rebuilt on rupture — where institutions have failed so visibly that the people who live here have learned, repeatedly, to build from the rubble. It is also a city of artists, migrants, night workers, and researchers who have consistently chosen presence over performance.

We are not romanticising a city. We are noting that context matters. Dyad starts here because the people here are already doing the thing — showing up, talking to strangers, building trust across difference. We are building infrastructure for something that already exists.

The conversation as the unit

Not the post. Not the profile. Not the follower count. The conversation: two people in a room, for an hour, actually trying. This is the irreducible unit of what we are building. Everything else — the prompt, the matching, the feedback — is scaffolding around that hour.

We Start
From Here.
p. 06dyad. Communities in Collective OwnershipWe Start From Here
(06) — Why In-Person, Why Now
"The question is not whether in-person contact matters. The question is why we stopped building infrastructure for it."

The infrastructure gap

We have excellent infrastructure for broadcasting, for searching, for consuming. We have almost none for the thing that most reliably produces wellbeing, civic trust, and social cohesion: repeated, genuine, face-to-face contact with people unlike ourselves.

Libraries got this right for books. Parks got this right for bodies. We have not yet built the equivalent for conversation.

Building from within the system

We are not waiting for ideal conditions. We are building with the tools available — imperfect platforms, imperfect ownership structures, imperfect people, in an imperfect city — because the alternative is not building at all.

Starting from within the system is not the same as accepting its terms. It is using the system's own infrastructure to build the thing that does not yet exist within it.

(07) — Chapter 03
What we are building instead.

The loop

01
Prompt. A member writes a question or topic they want to explore in conversation. Not a profile. A genuine opener.
02
Response. Another member responds in writing. The response is the context for the meeting — not a chat, not a date, not a pitch.
03
Invitation. The prompt author proposes a time and place. A public location in the city.
04
Meeting. Two people. One hour. A real place.
05
Feedback. Both parties complete a form before returning to the platform. Not a rating — a reflection.

Model.
p. 08dyad. Communities in Collective OwnershipThe Model
(08) — What We Refuse

What we measure

We do not measure daily active users, session duration, or content engagement. We track: conversations completed, members who showed up, feedback submitted, and — over time — whether members report that the conversations were worth having.

A platform that produces fewer, better conversations is succeeding. A platform that produces more, shallower ones has failed regardless of what the dashboard says.

What we refuse to optimise for

Time on platform

The goal is to get people off the platform and into a room together. More time spent in the app is a warning sign.

Network size

A large, shallow network is not the goal. A smaller, densely connected community that actually meets is.

Viral growth

We grow by invitation. Deliberate, curated, accountable. Not by making it easy to recruit strangers at scale.

(09) — Chapter 04
How we agree to be with one another.

Community-authored, not platform-imposed

These standards are not rules handed down by a platform to a user base. They are agreements a community makes with itself, maintained and revised by the People's Assembly. Community-authored standards have legitimacy that platform-imposed rules do not.

Floor protections

Harassment — cannot be weakened

Any behaviour intended to intimidate, threaten, or cause distress results in immediate removal. No warning.

Sexual exploitation — cannot be weakened

Using the platform to proposition, coerce, or exploit members sexually results in immediate permanent removal.

Intersectional abuse — cannot be weakened

Targeting someone for the combination of their identities — as a Black woman, as a trans woman of colour — is covered explicitly. We do not require you to prove which single category applies.

Not Neutral.

(10) — Partnership: Glitch
We work with Glitch — UK charity No. 1187714 — committed to ending online abuse and making the internet safer for everyone, unapologetically centring Black women. Research by Gabriela de Oliveira and Dr Julia Slupska informs our standards.

Five calls to action for tech companies

01
Responsibility at scale. Build moderation capacity proportional to your reach, not your growth targets.
02
Moderation beyond removal. Invest in understanding harm — not just reacting to it.
03
Intersectionality in policy. Single-axis moderation misses abuse at the intersection. Design for compound targeting.
04
The 10% distinction. Approximately 10% of content flagged as toxic is discussion of harmful tropes by those most affected. Protect that discussion.
05
Policy stability. Rollback of safety commitments — as seen on X/Twitter — is a harm, not a cost-saving measure.

(11) — Community Moderation in Practice
Blacksky built what the platforms wouldn't.

Blacksky is a Black-led community on Bluesky that created their own moderation label system. Labels are annotations on users and content — used to hide, warn, and categorise across the network. Seven categories. Configurable by each user. None exist in platform defaults.

(12) — What the Label Shows
"Misogynoir" is not a category in Twitter's content policy. It is not in Meta's Hateful Conduct guidelines. It is not in any platform default. Blacksky created it.

What naming makes possible

The label names something that existed before it did. Moya Bailey coined the word in 2008 to describe anti-Black misogyny — hatred that is simultaneously racist and sexist, and that single-axis systems cannot see. No major platform used it as a moderation category. A Black-led community did.

The label gives users a lever the platform never gave them: the ability to say what kind of harm they are experiencing, and to choose how to see it. Off. Warn. Hide. Not one-size-fits-all removal — configurable, by the people most affected.

What this means for us

Our floor protection on intersectional abuse is shaped by the same insight: compound targeting requires compound recognition. We do not ask people to prove which single axis applies. We name the thing as a whole.

Steward Ownershipdyad. Communities in Collective OwnershipChapter 05
(11) — Chapter 05
Why ownership shapes what technology becomes.
"The incentive structure is the product."

The equity trap

Venture capital is a contract. It says: grow, exit, return a multiple. Everything built under that contract is shaped by it — features that boost engagement, metrics that look good to acquirers, decisions that serve investors over users.

Dyad was not built to exit. There is no acquisition scenario we are optimising for. The goal is a self-sustaining community, governed by the people who use it, for as long as it is useful.

What steward ownership means

Steward ownership separates profit rights from control rights. Profits can flow to investors and workers — but control stays with people who are accountable to the mission. The company cannot be sold to a conflicting acquirer. The mission cannot be voted away.

p. 12dyad. Communities in Collective OwnershipSteward Ownership
(12) — What We Have Locked

The eighteen-month commitment

We are committing to completing the transition to steward ownership within eighteen months of community activation. The transition will be anchored by the Purpose Foundation — a German nonprofit specialising in steward ownership structures.

Locked commitments

No advertising, ever.

Not now, not under different leadership, not through a subsidiary. Legal constraint after transition.

No sale of member data.

Cannot be sold, licensed for commercial use, or used for advertising targeting.

No platform sale to a conflicting acquirer.

After transition, any acquisition requires consent of the Purpose Foundation anchor and the People's Assembly.

Community governance preserved.

The People's Assembly cannot be dissolved, suspended, or replaced by an appointed body.

(13) — Chapter 06
How decisions are made and power is distributed.

The People's Assembly

The People's Assembly is the primary governance body. All members are eligible to participate. The Assembly proposes changes, deliberates in public, and votes. Stewards — currently the founders — can also propose changes, but those proposals go through the same Assembly process.

Dual voting

Some decisions require a double majority: a majority of all votes cast, and a majority of the votes from the most affected group. This prevents any majority from overriding the interests of a specific community the decision most directly affects.

The amendment process

01
Any member may propose an amendment in writing.
02
A minimum 14-day deliberation period before any vote.
03
Standard changes: simple majority. Core commitments: supermajority.
04
Floor protections cannot be weakened. The Assembly can only strengthen them.

(15) — Chapter 07
What this is in service to, beyond the platform itself.

The wider question

Dyad is one attempt at one question: can you build social technology that serves the people who use it, rather than the people who built it? We do not know the full answer. We know some of the structural preconditions: ownership accountable to mission, governance genuinely participatory, moderation community-authored and intersectionally aware.

Place yourselves alongside

—
Libraries and reading rooms — public infrastructure for private thought
—
Credit unions — cooperative financial infrastructure that preceded banking reform
—
Free software — proving that commons-based production can outcompete proprietary models
—
Wikipedia — a proof of concept that a self-governed community can build something better than any corporation

(16) — What We Hope Others Build
"Prefigurative politics: the attempt to build the world you want inside the shell of the old one."

A template, not a monopoly

We are not building to become the only platform. We are building to demonstrate that a different kind of platform is possible — and to make the structure, the governance documents, and the hard-won knowledge available to anyone who wants to build something similar.

The goal is a network of city-based conversation platforms, each governed by its own community, each adapted to its own context. Berlin is the first. Not the last.

If not now, when

We are not waiting for better conditions. The conditions do not improve by waiting. Every year that passes is another year in which the dominant platforms consolidate, the alternatives become harder to sustain, and the people who might have built something different burn out trying.

We start now, with what we have, knowing it is incomplete. The incompleteness is not a reason to wait. It is the starting point.
