(03) — About This Document
What this is, what it is not, and how to use it.
Working draft. This document represents our current thinking, commitments, and understanding of what we are building and why. It will change. It is not a contract or a guarantee of specific future actions. It is an honest account, written to be held up against what we actually do. If there is a gap between this document and our behaviour, that gap is the accountability mechanism.

Dyad is a network for face-to-face conversations in Berlin. Behind that simple sentence is a long set of decisions — about ownership, governance, incentives, and the kind of infrastructure real human connection requires. These documents make those decisions legible.

This is more than an operational document. It is a blueprint and a manifesto for a different way of building a platform — one that treats the people who use it as co-owners of what it becomes, not as the product being sold.

Artists and culture workers, community organisers, and platform users often shy away from conversations about governance structures and ownership models. That discomfort is understandable. But if we want to build different kinds of platforms, we have to demystify the structures that shape them. These documents are an attempt at that.

How this document changes

Amendments to individual sections go through the People's Assembly — the community governance body described in the Governance document. The Assembly can propose changes, deliberate, and vote. Some commitments are foundational and will be permanently locked in the legal structure of the company when we complete our transition to steward ownership. Until then, they are binding public obligations.

The stewards — currently the founders — can also propose changes, but those proposals go through the same Assembly process. No one has unilateral authority to rewrite the framework.

Our Charter

The platforms we use to meet, organise, and make sense of the world have become engines of extraction. Not by accident. By design. The March 2026 verdicts against Meta and YouTube confirmed what years of research had already made plain: platform design is intentional, and it is liable to its effects. Attention economies are attention economies on purpose.

The typical response to this is ethical commitment. Companies publish values pages. Then they take equity investment, which obligates growth, which requires engagement optimisation, which means the values page was always a marketing document. Bandcamp was beloved. Then it was sold — twice, in eighteen months — and the community it served was not in the room when those decisions were made.

Dyad exists because we believe the structural problem requires a structural answer. This charter is that answer stated plainly.

(06) — The Problem & What Dyad Is

The problem we are responding to

Social platforms are built to maximise time-on-screen. This is not a side effect — it is the revenue model. Advertising pays for attention; platforms compete to capture it; every feed, notification, and recommendation system reflects this competition. The result is platforms optimised to make you feel connected while systematically replacing the conditions for real connection with their simulacra.

Cory Doctorow coined "enshittification" to describe how platforms decline: first they serve users, then business customers, then extract maximum value from both. The community has no say. It was never in the ownership structure.

What dyad is

A place to find someone to talk to, in person. You write a question or a subject you want to explore. Someone reads it and writes back. If both of you want to meet, you agree on a time and a place. Then you show up. That is the whole thing.

The technology exists to make this as safe, trustworthy, and low-friction as possible — not to grow, not to engage, not to retain. Dyad gets out of the way once it has done its job of getting two people into the same room. The absence of feeds, algorithms, social graphs, and engagement mechanics is not a missing feature set. It is a deliberate structural decision.

(07) — Something to say yes to
Something to say yes to
Ownership by the people who use it
Dyad is committed to transitioning to steward ownership — a legal structure in which voting control stays with the people who operate the platform, not external investors. Our pre-seed funding instrument is designed explicitly to make this transition possible.
Aligned incentives, not managed conflicts
Subscription funding means dyad succeeds when members value the platform enough to pay for it — not when they cannot stop scrolling. No advertising. No data sales. No engagement optimisation.
Governance by the community
Active members govern this platform through the People's Assembly — a real decision-making body, not an advisory board. Community standards, acceptance criteria, and platform policy are set and changed by the members who live by them.
Transparency as practice, not performance
We publish the financials. We explain the decisions. When we change course, we say so and explain why. Annual financial reports are available to all members.
Connection as the endpoint, not the product
Two people in a room having a real conversation is not a means to a platform metric. It is the thing itself. Dyad is designed around the quality of that encounter, not the quantity of time spent on screen before it.

(08) — Our real competition

Our real competition

Our competition is not another app. It is cynicism — the well-earned belief that structural change in how platforms are built and owned is not actually possible. That collective governance doesn't scale. That mission statements always give way to investor returns.

We understand this position. Cynicism is a rational response to repeated betrayal. But it is also an obstacle. We are competing against:

The notion that platforms inevitably extract value from the communities they serve
The assumption that investment requires surrendering governance to investors
The belief that social connection cannot be supported by infrastructure that does not exploit it
The comfort of the status quo, even when it is clearly not working

(09) — What we commit to / What we ask

What we commit to you

Your data is yours

We collect only what is necessary to run the platform. We do not sell it, analyse it for advertising, or share it with third parties for commercial purposes.

No advertising, ever

Not now, not later, not under different leadership, not through a subsidiary. This will be encoded in the legal form of the company.

Radical transparency

We publish the financials. We explain our decisions. Annual financial reports are available to all members.

Real governance, not advisory theatre

The People's Assembly is not a focus group. Active members vote. Decisions bind us.

A path to structural ownership

Steward ownership transition within eighteen months, conditional on community activation.

What we ask of you

Show up

If you agree to meet someone, be there. The trust is the infrastructure. No-shows corrode it.

Be honest

Not polished. Honest. The person across the table is taking the same risk. They deserve the real version of you.

Engage in good faith

The encounter does not have to be easy or end in agreement. It has to be genuine.

Participate in governance

Once active — five or more meetings completed — you have a vote in the People's Assembly. Use it.

Membership Agreement

Membership is not a purchase. It is an entry into a mutual relationship — one that carries rights, responsibilities, and a real say in how this community governs itself. Governance rights are earned through participation, not purchased through subscription.

What membership is

Full access to the platform: writing conversation prompts, responding to others', sending and receiving meeting invitations, and participating in the feedback cycle that follows every meeting.

Active members — those who have completed at least five meetings — have the right to participate in the People's Assembly and vote on community governance decisions. This is not a passive entitlement. It is a responsibility that comes with the platform.

Not a User.
A Member.

p. 12dyad.Membership — Commitments & Endings
(12) — Commitments & How Membership Ends

What you commit to

The community standards

By becoming a member, you agree to the community standards. These are not fine print. Read them.

Showing up

If you agree to meet someone, show up. No-shows are taken seriously and affect the reputation others see when they consider meeting you.

Completing the feedback form

After every meeting, you complete a feedback form before accessing the platform again. This is a hard gate. The feedback system is how the community maintains accountability over time. There is no bypass.

Your identity

One person, one account. A pseudonym is fine. But creating multiple accounts to escape a reputation, circumvent a suspension, or game the system is grounds for permanent removal.

Your data

We collect the minimum necessary to run the platform. We do not sell your data. We do not use it for advertising. You can request a full export at any time and delete your account at any time.

How membership ends

If you choose to leave: Cancel at any time. No penalty, no exit fee. If you delete your account, the other party in any pending meetings is notified and released.

If dyad ends your membership: For violations of the community standards. Most cases begin with a warning. Serious violations — harassment, sexual exploitation, ban evasion — result in immediate removal without warning.

Community Standards

These standards are not rules handed down from a platform to a user base. They are agreements a community makes with itself. Some can be changed by the People's Assembly. Some cannot be changed at all.

Floor protections — cannot be weakened

Harassment — immediate removal
Any behaviour intended to intimidate, threaten, or cause distress — before, during, or after a meeting — results in immediate removal. No warning. The People's Assembly can strengthen this. It cannot weaken it.
Sexual exploitation — immediate removal
Using the platform to proposition, coerce, or exploit other members sexually results in permanent removal and, where appropriate, referral to relevant authorities.
Intersectional and identity-based abuse — floor protection
Targeting someone for the combination of their identities — as a Black woman, as a trans woman of colour, as any person targeted for who they are as a whole — is covered explicitly. You do not need to prove which single category of harm applies. Our standards on this are informed by our partnership with Glitch.

Show up — or cancel with notice
If you agree to meet, be there. Cancellations with 12+ hours' notice are least consequential. No-shows are treated as a serious breach.
One account. One identity.
Creating additional accounts to escape a reputation or circumvent a suspension is grounds for permanent removal.
Complete the feedback form
After every meeting, before accessing the platform again. No bypass. This is how accountability is maintained.

p. 13dyad.Community Standards — Glitch

We work with Glitch — UK charity No. 1187714, committed to ending online abuse and making the internet a safer place for everyone, unapologetically centring Black women. The calls to action on this page are drawn from their research, co-authored by Gabriela de Oliveira and Dr Julia Slupska.

Calls to action for tech companies

On responsibility at scale

Tech companies invented neither patriarchy nor white supremacy, but they are responsible for the scale and spread of their digital expression. That responsibility includes what gets amplified, what gets removed, and what the architecture makes inevitable. It cannot be outsourced.

On moderation beyond removal

Content moderation cannot be limited to removing posts. It must extend to actively promoting communities and positive connections — not only suppressing harm but building the conditions in which people are safe.

On intersectionality in policy

When someone is targeted for two identities at once — for being Black and a woman in the same act — single-axis policies fail. Glitch found this in practice: Twitter's Dehumanisation Policy missed images targeting a dark-skinned Black woman because the harm fell between its categories. Policy must protect people as they actually are.

The 10% distinction

In Glitch's dataset, approximately 10% of flagged 'toxic' content was not abuse — it was discussion of harmful tropes. Moderation that cannot tell the difference removes the spaces where Black women name and resist what is being done to them. Protecting those spaces is part of the work.

On policy stability

Twitter's quiet removal in 2023 of its policy prohibiting deadnaming and misgendering trans people is a warning. Progressive safety policies are reversible under new ownership. Civil society, journalism, and community governance are what make rollback costly enough to resist.

Governance

A platform is not corrupted by bad people. It is corrupted by a structure that makes bad outcomes inevitable. When a company is financed through equity, it is obligated to grow. When growth plateaus, it extracts. This is not a story about founders losing their way — it is a structure doing exactly what it was designed to do.

Dyad is built to be different in structure, not just in intention. This document explains that structure: where we are now, what we have committed to, how governance works, and what it will mean for members when the transition to steward ownership is complete.

Five things simultaneously — not as aspirations, but as structural properties encoded into the form of the company itself.
01
No investor governance, ever. People who provide capital have no say in how the platform is run, what it optimises for, or who it can be sold to. Guaranteed by the steward ownership structure — not by good intentions that can be revised when they become inconvenient.
02
Profit serves purpose, not the reverse. Surplus generated by the platform is reinvested into the platform, distributed to workers as reasonable compensation, or used to subsidise the community pricing tier. Investor returns are capped and defined before capital is received.
03
Community governs community affairs. Standards, criteria, and policy are set by the People's Assembly — not by the platform's founders or its investors.
04
No sale without community consent. After the steward ownership transition, any acquisition requires consent of the Purpose Foundation anchor and the People's Assembly.
05
Sustainable without exploitation. Success is measured by whether members value the platform enough to pay for it — not by time-on-screen, data value, or advertising revenue.

Governance — Structural Problemdyad.p. 15
(19) — The Structural Problem
Problem
Misaligned incentives

Equity-financed platforms are structurally obligated to prioritise investor returns. When users and investors want different things, investors win. This is governance working as designed.

Problem
Value extraction

Platforms serve users, then business customers, then extract maximum value from both. The community has no structural recourse.

Problem
Governance without accountability

Platform communities generate enormous value — content, data, trust, time. But they have no formal claim on the decisions that affect them.

The design challenge
Capital + control

Combining cooperative control with functional capital access, without surrendering governance to investors in exchange. This is the problem dyad's structure is built to solve.

Why existing alternatives fall short

Traditional cooperatives solve the governance problem but struggle with capital access. They cannot issue equity, limiting external investment. Platforms — where network effects reward the first mover — are a particularly difficult environment for co-op formation.

Equity crowdfunding extends ownership to more people, but governance rights track money, not engagement. This reproduces the same fundamental alignment problem: governance stake accumulates to capital, not participation.

p. 16dyad.Governance — Alternatives & Where We Are
(20) — Alternatives & Where We Are

Tokens and DAOs

Token-based governance promised community ownership but largely delivered governance theatre. Most token holders have no meaningful mechanism to exercise governance, and the structures that exist tend to be captured by large holders who acquired stakes financially rather than through participation.

Profit sharing

Distributing returns to workers or users addresses the distribution problem without addressing the control problem. A platform can pay out profits while remaining fully controlled by its equity holders. Profit sharing does not give recipients a voice in how the platform is run. It is better than nothing. It is not governance.

Steward ownership is the structure that attempts to solve both capital access and cooperative control simultaneously. It is not a complete solution. It is the best available one.

Where we are now

Dyad is currently raising pre-seed funding. Investors accept from the outset that the company will transition to a steward-owned structure. They do not hold traditional equity. They will have no governance claim after the transition. This is agreed before capital is received — not asked for afterwards.

Steward ownership explained

In a steward-owned company, voting control stays permanently with the people who operate it. It does not transfer to investors. Profits are not extracted through equity — investors receive capped returns, and their financial claim then ends. The legal structure makes this binding. Reference companies: Ecosia, Einhorn, and Waschbär — all operating under the German Verantwortungseigentum framework.

Steward Ownership.
Governance — Funding & Dual Votingdyad.p. 17
(21) — Funding & Dual Voting

Steward ownership is not a values statement. It is a legal constraint. The distinction matters: values can be revised; legal structures require a structural act to undo. Our commitment is to make these things the same — to encode our values into the structure so that no future change in personnel or investor composition can override them.

How we are funded

Pre-seed capital through a funding instrument designed specifically for companies on the steward ownership path. Investors enter knowing and agreeing to the transition. No traditional equity. Capped returns. No ongoing governance claim. No veto over operational decisions.

Dual voting

Dyad operates two parallel governance tracks. Both exclude investors. Together they constitute the full governance of the platform.

Track One
Steward governance

Corporate structure, financial decisions, major strategic changes, stewardship succession. Voting held by the stewards — currently the founders. Investors have no vote here. Guaranteed by the legal structure.

Track Two
People's Assembly

Community standards, acceptance criteria, membership policy, platform features that affect community life. Voting belongs to active members. Investors have no vote here either. One active member, one vote.

Two Tracks. Zero Investors.
p. 18dyad.Governance — People's Assembly
(22) — The People's Assembly

The People's Assembly

The People's Assembly is not appointed by us. It is constituted by any active member who chooses to participate. Showing up to the Assembly is itself an act of governance.

What the Assembly does

Reviews and votes on proposed changes to community standards, acceptance criteria, and the membership agreement. Proposes and votes on new policies. Reviews appeals of governance decisions. Monitors enforcement. Holds stewards accountable through public record.

How it works

Asynchronous, in writing, via a structured deliberation space accessible to all active members. Any active member can submit a proposal — requires a second before entering deliberation. Standard proposals: 7-day minimum deliberation. Major proposals: 14-day minimum. Standard decisions: simple majority. Major decisions: supermajority (two-thirds).

Three tiers of decision-making

Operational — Day-to-day platform operations: moderation, customer support, feature development within existing policy. Handled by the team. Communicated in monthly updates to the Assembly.

Strategic — Pricing changes, expansion to new cities, partnerships, significant product direction. Proposed by Stewards with a 14-day Assembly response window.

Foundational — Core commitments, governance structure, legal form. Some require Assembly supermajority. Others — no advertising, no data sales, steward ownership itself — are permanently locked in the legal structure after transition. No vote can change them.

(24) — A prism of possibilities
What the structure makes possible

Unlike venture-backed platforms — which have a single trajectory toward growth, exit, and extraction — dyad's cooperative structure preserves multiple futures. The goal is self-sustained operation: a community that pays for the infrastructure it values, governed by the people who use it, indefinitely.

—
Continued self-sustained operation, funded by membership, governed by the Assembly
—
Federation with other city-based conversation networks as dyad opens in new cities
—
Exit to nonprofit or foundation stewardship, preserving the mission permanently
—
Any transition: democratic participation, broad distribution of benefits, member negotiation power

Stages of success

Stage 1 — Platform viability. A community that values dyad enough to pay for it. Operational surplus. The first People's Assembly convenes.

Stage 2 — Governance proven. The Assembly proposes and passes its first changes. Members experience governance as real, not symbolic. Steward ownership transition complete.

Stage 3 — Cultural influence. Dyad is cited as evidence that community-owned social infrastructure works. Other builders reference the model.

Stage 4 — Institutional durability. The platform outlasts its founders. Governance passes to a new generation of members. The structure is doing its job.
