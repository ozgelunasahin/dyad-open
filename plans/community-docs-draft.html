Preface
About This Document
What this document is, what it is not, and why it exists.
Working draft. This is the editorial brief for dyad. — a publication about rebuilding the social infrastructure of cities, and the founding document of a platform built on the same principles. The two are connected: the publication argues the case; the platform builds it. This document will change. Its purpose is to be legible enough that the people who read it can say yes or no.

The social infrastructure of public life — the spaces where strangers can gradually become real to one another — has been systematically replaced by its digital simulation. This document is an attempt to understand why that happened, name what was lost, and propose what rebuilding looks like.

These sixteen essays form a diagnostic sequence and a constructive proposal. The first five chapters name the problem. Chapter VI defines what alternative-making actually means. The remaining ten build the case for what dyad is, why it is structured the way it is, and what it could become.

Chapter I
Natural History
How the Internet Lost Its Social Nervous System

Before the feed, the internet was participatory. Forums, blogs, comment sections, early social discovery — people making things and finding each other across them. This chapter traces how that changed: how a web built around connection was progressively redesigned for prediction, advertising, and behavioral management.

The shift was not inevitable. It was a series of decisions — about business models, about measurement, about what counts as success. Understanding those decisions is the first step toward making different ones.

Key themes

Forums, blogs, and early social discovery
The participatory web: making versus watching
How advertising became the dominant revenue model
Algorithmic feeds and the replacement of participation by spectatorship
What was lost when attention became the product

Chapter II
The Rupture
What Happens When People Can No Longer Metabolize Reality Together

The collapse of spaces where strangers can gradually become real to one another has consequences beyond loneliness. When encounter is replaced by spectacle — when we watch rather than meet — we lose the capacity to make sense of a shared world together. This chapter names what that loss actually looks like.

Parallel realities, performative identity, conversational exhaustion — these are not individual pathologies. They are structural effects of platforms designed to keep people engaged without ever requiring them to truly encounter anyone.

Key themes

Parallel realities and the collapse of shared ground
Performative identity versus the self you bring to a room
Conversational exhaustion and the cost of mediated sociality
What it means to encounter someone versus to observe them
The collapse of public encounter and what replaces it

Chapter III
Pathology
Systems Optimized for Engagement, Extraction, and Behavioral Prediction

This is not an argument about bad intentions. It is an argument about bad design. When profit depends on attention, every system — every feed, notification, and recommendation — is optimised to capture it. Surveillance capitalism, engagement loops, parasociality, black-box ranking: these are not side effects. They are the product.

Understanding the incentive structure of the platforms we live inside is not optional. It is the prerequisite for imagining anything different.

Key themes

Surveillance capitalism as a business model
Engagement loops and the design of compulsion
Parasociality: intimacy without reciprocity
Black-box ranking systems and invisible governance
Extraction architectures and what they optimize for

Chapter IV
Epidemiology
How Platform Architectures Reorganize Human Behavior and Public Life

The effects of engagement-optimised platforms do not stay inside the app. They spread: into politics, institutions, relationships, and the texture of everyday life. This chapter traces how digital architectures propagate behaviors across culture — and what it looks like when an entire society reorganizes itself around the logic of the feed.

Social fragmentation, polarization, distrust, civic fatigue — these are not individual choices. They are the downstream effects of systems that were optimised, at enormous scale, for something else entirely.

Key themes

Social fragmentation at scale
Polarization as a structural output, not an accident
Distrust and the erosion of shared epistemic ground
Civic fatigue and the collapse of collective agency
Behavioral normalization: how the strange becomes ordinary

Chapter V
The Limits of Reform
Why Better Feeds Cannot Repair Social Fragmentation

Moderation tweaks, healthier feeds, and ethical branding have a ceiling. The problem is not that platforms are run by bad people — it is that they are structured by bad incentives. Structural problems require structural solutions. Better intentions inside broken incentive systems produce the same outcomes.

Platform lock-in, optimization logic, incentive incompatibility: this chapter makes the case that reform fails not because reformers lack sincerity, but because the systems they are trying to reform are working exactly as designed — for someone else's purposes.

Key themes

Why platform lock-in makes reform from within nearly impossible
Optimization logic and why it always wins against stated values
Incentive incompatibility: what it means when good intentions aren't enough
The history of failed platform reform efforts
What an infrastructural critique requires that a product critique cannot provide

Chapter VII
Intervention
Designing for Encounter Instead of Engagement

The introduction of dyad's core mechanism. You write a question or a subject you want to explore. Someone reads it and writes back. If both of you want to meet, you agree on a time and a place. Then you show up. The goal is not to keep you on the platform — it is to get you off it, into a room with another person, as quickly as possible.

Optimising for time-to-offline is a different goal than any engagement platform has ever tried to achieve. This chapter explains the design logic behind it — and why minimizing mediation is a feature, not a missing feature set.

Key themes

Prompts as conversation starters, not content
Bounded interaction: what you can and cannot do on dyad
Minimizing mediation as a design principle
Designing for reciprocity rather than asymmetric attention
Optimizing for time-to-offline

Chapter IX
Protocols for Public Life
Conditions for Trust, Disagreement, and Human Encounter

Community standards reconceived as civic infrastructure rather than moderation policy. The norms that make real conversation possible — reciprocity, accountability, conversational courage, and the willingness to remain in a room with someone who sees the world differently — do not arise naturally. They have to be designed for.

This chapter argues that community standards are not rules imposed on a community from outside. They are the community's agreement about what kind of encounter it wants to make possible — and they require the same care as any other piece of civic infrastructure.

Key themes

Disagreement as a feature, not a failure mode
Reciprocity as the basis of trust
Accountability structures that serve the community, not the platform
Conversational courage: what it takes to stay present
Relational norms as infrastructure

Chapter XI
Governance
How Decisions Are Made and Power Is Distributed

The legal form of a platform is not a technical detail. It determines what the platform can become — and what it cannot. This chapter explains the governance structures behind dyad: steward ownership, dual voting, the People's Assembly. Not as aspirations, but as legal constraints.

A platform is not corrupted by bad people. It is corrupted by a structure that makes bad outcomes inevitable. This is a chapter about structure: where it comes from, why it matters, and how to build it so that good intentions do not need to carry the weight alone.

Key themes

Steward ownership: voting control stays with operators, not investors
Transparency: decisions documented, financials published
Democratic participation through the People's Assembly
Modular governance: operational, strategic, and foundational decisions separated
Public accountability as a legal constraint, not a promise

Chapter XIII
Berlin
A Prototype for Rebuilding Local Social Fabric

Why Berlin first. A city dense enough for the model to work, diverse enough to test it, and already organised around the kind of cultural scenes and community spaces that make face-to-face encounter feel imaginable. This chapter makes the case for Berlin as the right starting point — and maps what rebuilding local social infrastructure looks like at city scale.

Density, migration, cultural scenes, hybrid communities, local experimentation: Berlin is not chosen for convenience. It is chosen because it is one of the cities where the conditions for this kind of experiment are already present — and where the need for it is most visible.

Key themes

Urban density and why it matters for encounter systems
Migration, transience, and the specific social texture of Berlin
Cultural scenes as existing infrastructure for connection
Hybrid communities: people who belong to multiple worlds at once
Local experimentation as a methodology for building at scale

Chapter XV
Possibilities
What Becomes Possible When People Become Real to One Another Again

When the relational layer of society is rebuilt — when strangers can encounter one another again in conditions that allow for genuine presence — what becomes possible that currently is not? Civic trust, collective intelligence, democratic resilience, public culture: not as aspirations, but as structural consequences of a different kind of social infrastructure.

This is the final argument: that the work of rebuilding encounter systems is not marginal. It is foundational. The capacity to remain in genuine contact with people who are different from us is not a nice thing to have. It is the condition under which everything else we care about becomes possible.

Key themes

Civic trust as a structural outcome, not a value
Collective intelligence and what it requires to function
Democratic resilience and the social conditions that sustain it

Operational Agreements, Policies, and Governance Materials

The governing documents of dyad — the institutional infrastructure that makes everything else possible. These are not fine print. They are the agreements through which a community governs itself, holds itself accountable, and makes its values legible to the people who live by them.
